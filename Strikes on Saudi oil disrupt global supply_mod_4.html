SAUDI OIL UNDER ATTACK

Strikes on Saudi oil disrupt global supply

Published Sept 16, 2019 | Updated Sept 19, 2019

The assault on oil facilities in the heart of Saudi Arabia’s energy industry triggered the biggest intraday rise in crude prices since 1991. The attack, which Saudi Arabia has blamed on arch-foe Iran, laid bare the vulnerability of one of the world’s biggest oil suppliers.

Satellite imagery showed several plumes of smoke at a facility belonging to state oil giant Saudi Aramco in the eastern city of Abqaiq, the world’s biggest oil processing plant. Operations there are critical for global oil flows.

The September 14 attack initially knocked out more than half of the country’s oil production, although Riyadh has since said it would quickly restore supplies.

Since May, several attacks have targeted oil facilities on Saudi soil and on oil tankers in Gulf waters.

A crucial facility

The oil-processing plant of Abqaiq is located 60 km (37 miles) southwest of Aramco’s Dhahran headquarters. It handles crude from the world’s largest conventional oilfield, the supergiant Ghawar, and exports to terminals Ras Tanura — the world’s biggest offshore oil loading facility — and Juaymah. It also pumps westwards across the kingdom to export terminals on the Red Sea.

Khurais, 190 km (118 miles) further southwest, contains the country’s second largest oilfield, was also damaged in the attacks.

The attacks cut the kingdom’s output by 5.7m barrels per day (bpd), according to a statement from Aramco. This represents more than 5% of global oil supply and 27% of OPEC's production. Saudi Arabia said the lost output would be fully restored by the end of the month.

Note: The 11 countries shown in the graphic have OPEC production targets. A further three members – Iran, Libya and Venezuela – are excluded from production caps

Before the attack, the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries' (OPEC) global supply cushion was just over 3.21 million barrels per day (bpd). Saudi Arabia — the de facto leader of OPEC — had 2.27 million bpd of that capacity. Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates hold most of the remaining 940,000 bpd of spare capacity.

Who could be behind the attack?

Yemen’s Iran-aligned Houthi movement claimed responsibility. U.S. and Saudi officials rejected the claim, saying the attack had not come from the south.

In an attempt to support its assertion that Iran was responsible, Saudi Arabia displayed the remains of some of the drones and missiles it said were used.

Remains of the missiles which the Saudi government says were used to attack an Aramco oil facility, are displayed during a news conference in Riyadh, September 18, 2019. (REUTERS/Hamad I Mohammed)

Saudi officials explained that an investigation into where the attacks were launched from was still underway and the result would be announced at a later date. Iran has dismissed the allegations that it was responsible.

U.S. President Donald Trump said on September 18 that there were many options for how to respond short of going to war with Iran. He ordered increased sanctions on Tehran.

Proxy influences suspected

The heightened tensions of late have stoked concerns about increasing bloodshed in countries where Iran and Saudi Arabia are locked in a battle for regional supremacy. The countries are widely believed to fund and train proxy groups in Bahrain, Iraq, Lebanon, Qatar, Syria and Yemen.
